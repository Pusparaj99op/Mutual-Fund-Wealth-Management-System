What is Monte Carlo Simulation?

Monte Carlo Simulation is a computational algorithm that uses repeated random sampling to obtain numerical results. Named after the Monte Carlo Casino in Monaco, this technique allows us to model the probability of different outcomes in a process that cannot easily be predicted due to the intervention of random variables.

In FIMFP: We run 10,000 simulation paths using Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM) to predict future NAV values and calculate risk metrics like Value at Risk (VaR) for mutual funds.

Mathematical Foundation

The core of our Monte Carlo simulation is based on Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM), which models stock price movements with the following stochastic differential equation:

Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)
$$dS_t = \mu S_t \, dt + \sigma S_t \, dW_t$$

The discrete-time approximation used for simulation is:

Discrete-Time GBM
$$S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \times \exp\left[\left(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)\Delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} \, Z\right]$$

Where \( Z \sim N(0,1) \) is a standard normal random variable.

Risk Metrics Calculated

Value at Risk (VaR) measures the maximum expected loss over a given time period at a specified confidence level:

Value at Risk (VaR) at 95% Confidence
$$VaR_{95\%} = \text{Percentile}_5(\text{Portfolio Returns})$$

Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR), also known as Expected Shortfall:

Conditional VaR (Expected Shortfall)
$$CVaR_{95\%} = E[\text{Loss} \,|\, \text{Loss} > VaR_{95\%}]$$

Probability of Loss calculation:

Probability of Loss
$$P(\text{Loss}) = \frac{\text{Number of simulations with } S_T < S_0}{\text{Total simulations}}$$
